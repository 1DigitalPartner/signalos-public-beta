# SignalOS Product Roadmap

This roadmap describes the current direction of the SignalOS controlled public beta.

It is intentionally outcome-driven. Items are prioritized according to product safety, commercial value, tester feedback, and operational readiness.

The roadmap is not a fixed delivery contract. Scope and sequence may change as the beta produces new evidence.

---

## Product direction

SignalOS is being developed as a growth intelligence and controlled execution platform for founders, agencies, consultants, marketers, developers, and B2B teams.

The product direction is based on four core outcomes:

1. detect commercially important growth gaps;
2. explain those gaps clearly;
3. convert approved findings into execution-ready work;
4. apply and verify changes through controlled, auditable workflows.

---

## Roadmap principles

SignalOS development follows these principles:

- safety before autonomy;
- evidence before recommendation;
- exact targets before external writes;
- review before execution;
- verification after real execution;
- commercial usefulness over feature volume;
- simple language over unnecessary complexity;
- multi-source intelligence over isolated scans;
- reusable workflows over one-off outputs;
- auditability and rollback by default.

## Phase 1 — Public beta readiness

### Objective

Make SignalOS safe, understandable, and testable by a controlled external audience.

### Priorities

- complete public beta documentation;
- publish guided Explorer and Operator missions;
- create structured issue templates;
- create product feedback templates;
- create connector and Premium Agent test templates;
- establish a private security reporting process;
- add screenshots and end-to-end evidence;
- provide a clear beta access request path;
- define tester cohorts;
- define beta allowances;
- add beta onboarding messages;
- improve empty states and locked states;
- validate mobile and desktop usability;
- improve user-facing error handling;
- verify rate limiting and abuse controls.

### Exit criteria

- external testers can understand the product without direct explanation;
- testers can complete an asset scan;
- Operator testers can complete a safe GitHub execution;
- all external writes remain approval-gated;
- security-sensitive feedback has a private route;
- feedback is structured enough to drive product decisions.

## Phase 4 — Connector expansion

### Objective

Support additional controlled execution targets without weakening the approval model.

### Candidate connectors

- GitHub;
- GitLab;
- Vercel;
- Cloudflare;
- Google Analytics;
- Google Search Console;
- email platforms;
- CRM platforms;
- project management systems;
- content management systems;
- advertising platforms;
- data warehouses.

### Connector requirements

Every write-capable connector should support:

- explicit authorization;
- revocation;
- exact target configuration;
- target-change approval invalidation;
- separate application approval;
- preview before execution;
- execution metadata;
- verification;
- rollback guidance;
- audit records.

A connector will not be released merely because an API integration is technically possible.

## Explicitly not prioritized

SignalOS will not prioritize:

- unrestricted autonomous production writes;
- feature volume without clear value;
- generic AI chat as the primary product;
- opaque recommendations;
- silent connector actions;
- direct protected-branch writes;
- unreviewable automation;
- vanity dashboards without operational use;
- integrations without a clear security model;
- placeholder-quality generated work.

---

## How roadmap decisions are made

Roadmap decisions consider:

- security risk;
- tester evidence;
- commercial value;
- frequency of the use case;
- implementation cost;
- operational complexity;
- connector reliability;
- measurable outcomes;
- design partner demand;
- strategic differentiation.

Requests with a real workflow, expected outcome, and business impact are more useful than general feature suggestions.

## Public status labels

Roadmap items may use these labels:

- `Exploring` — problem and demand are being evaluated;
- `Planned` — accepted direction, scope still being defined;
- `In development` — active implementation;
- `Private beta` — limited testing;
- `Public beta` — external controlled testing;
- `Released` — generally available;
- `Deferred` — valid but not currently prioritized;
- `Declined` — not aligned with product direction.
